Vectors Frame 1

In physics there are quantities, such as temperature and time, where it is sufficient to magnitude and some units. For example, The coffee is \(205^{o}F\) or it took me \(10\hspace{0.2cm}s\) to get to the mailbox. These quantities are called scalars

Imagine telling me how to get to your house, starting from my house. If you tell me that your house is two miles from my house, then all that I know is that your house is somewhere on the circumference of a circle of radius two miles with my house at the center

On the other hand, if you tell me the distance and direction from my house to your house I will be able to exactly locate it. For example, if you told me your house is two miles northeast of my house (specifically \(\theta\) degrees north of east) I would be able to find it.

Vectors Frame 2

A vector is a quantity that has a magnitude and direction. Vectors can be represented as an arrow in space. The magnitude (amount) is represented as the length of the arrow, and the direction is represented by the angle formed between the x-axis or the y-axis.

A vector in the x-y plane has two components. Namely, an x-component and a y-component. We can think of the x-component of a vector as the shadow of the vector on the x-axis. Likewise for the y-component and the y-axis.

Since the angle \(\theta\) is formed between the hypotenuse and the x-axis, \(F_x\) is the side adjacent to \(\theta \), and \(F_x = |\vec{F}|cos(\theta)\)

Since \(F_x\) is the side adjacent to \(\theta \), \(F_y\) must be the side opposite to \(\theta\), and \(F_y = |\vec{F}|sin(\theta)\)
